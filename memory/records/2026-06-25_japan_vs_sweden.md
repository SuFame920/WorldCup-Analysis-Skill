### ⭐ Reflection — THE CALIBRATION SYSTEM'S FINEST HOUR

**This match is the textbook justification for calibration rule #9 (爆冷→option hedging).**

All 4 hits were the **secondary/hedge** picks. Without the mandatory dual-pick rule (爆冷≥20%→强制双选), this would have been a **0/5 or 1/5 disaster**. Every single hedge landed — the system actively prevented a wipeout.

**5-whys:**
1. **Why did all hedges hit?** The pre-match assessment of ~25% upset probability was accurate. Sweden's elite attackers (Isak, Elanga) delivered: Elanga's world-class equalizer was pure individual quality. Japan scored first but couldn't hold the lead against Sweden's desperate push.
2. **What was the core mechanism?** Asymmetric motivation dynamics: Sweden HAD to attack after going behind (Maeda 56'), and they had the individual quality to convert. Japan's "counter-attacking" identity worked for the goal but couldn't protect the lead against sustained pressure. Isak's crossbar in stoppage nearly won it for Sweden.
3. **Why did 半全场 miss?** The match was heading for 平平 until Japan's goal, and then Sweden's quick equalizer (6 minutes later) returned it to 平平. 平胜 would have needed Japan to hold the lead.
4. **What calibration was validated?** (a) 强制双选对冲 saved all 4 markets; (b) 让球 push认知 (putting 让负 as secondary); (c) cross-line totals coverage (3球+2球); (d) BTTS logic (both teams had scored in every WC game — pattern continued).
5. **Transfer:** This game is the gold-standard example of WHY the England 0-0 Ghana lesson was embedded as mandatory rule #9. When there's a specific upset mechanism and probability ≥20%, the entire prediction slate must reflect it — not just a note. This match proves the system learns and gets better.
